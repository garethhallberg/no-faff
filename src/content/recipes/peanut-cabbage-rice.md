This is the sort of thing you cook when you have a cabbage, a red onion, a chilli, and no desire to go shopping.

It is filling, vegetarian, and much better than it has any right to be.

## Ingredients

- 1 red onion, sliced
- 1/4 white cabbage, finely sliced
- 1 red chilli, chopped
- 1 thumb-sized piece of fresh ginger, grated or finely chopped
- 2 tablespoons peanut butter
- 1 tablespoon soy sauce
- 1 teaspoon vinegar or lime juice
- Splash of hot water
- Cooked basmati rice
- Oil for frying

## Method

### 1. Build the base

Fry the red onion in a little oil over a medium heat until softened and starting to sweeten.

Add the cabbage and cook until it loses its raw edge but still has a bit of bite.

### 2. Add the flavour

Add the chilli and ginger. Cook for another minute or two.

You want the ginger to wake up, not burn.

### 3. Make it saucy

Stir the peanut butter with soy sauce, vinegar or lime juice, and a splash of hot water.

Add it to the pan and stir until everything is coated.

Add more hot water if it gets too thick.

### 4. Balance it

Taste it.

- Too heavy? Add vinegar or lime.
- Too sharp? Add a tiny pinch of sugar.
- Too thick? Add hot water.
- Too bland? Add soy sauce.

Serve over basmati rice.

## No-faff swaps

- No fresh ginger? Use garlic instead.
- No chilli? Use chilli flakes.
- No cabbage? Use cauliflower, mushrooms, broccoli, or whatever needs using. If uisng cauliflower AND cabbage put the cauliflower in five minutes before the cabbage.
- Want protein? Add tofu, haricot beans, chickpeas, or edamame.
- Swap in brown onions if that's what you have.

## Why this works

The onion gives sweetness, the cabbage gives bulk, the peanut butter gives fat and richness, and the soy/vinegar combination stops it becoming claggy.

It is basically cupboard cooking, but with a bit of balance.

## Make it immense

Finish with any of these:

- sesame seeds
- spring onion
- crispy onions
- lime juice
- chilli oil
- crushed peanuts

## Everyday eating note

This is a good "end of the fridge" recipe. The point is not to follow it exactly. The point is to understand the shape of it:

**base → flavour → sauce → balance**
